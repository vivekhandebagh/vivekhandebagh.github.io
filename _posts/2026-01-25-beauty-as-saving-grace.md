There is a strange gravitational pull that the aesthetic experience exerts upon the human psyche. The existence of this force reveals that beauty is not just an ornament or an accessory to the experience of life, but it is a fundamental element of the structure of existence. 

Consider how we move through our days: most of it is us navigating from task to task, solving problems, meeting deadlines, accumulating resources, and following the biological forces within us. But sometimes, we end up pausing before a sunset as we walk back to our car after work. Doing so doesn’t serve any utalitarian goal. Rather, something about that particular marriage of light and color speaks to us. It is simply beautiful. 

In articulating the aesthetic experience, even in just the limited capacity that I have done above, you are already alluded to an intuition that there is some relationship between beauty and the realm of the transcendent. When we stand before a masterwork of art, we experience what might be called "ontological vertigo.” It’s that dizzying state where the individual recognizes that reality contains depths they seldom see. A momentary state where the ground underneath your goals, ambitions, and mental constructs crumbles away. The aesthetic experience pierces the veil of your mundane reality, and it quickly fills the vacuum with its own presence. A sort of saving grace.

However, what is perhaps the more vital insight here is that we are not the mere recievers of the aesthetic experience, but we are also active agents that can be responsible for it. When we arrange paintings on our walls, choose clothes that please us, and seek out music that moves us, we are choosing to rebel against the tyranny of a purely functional existence. Through our aesthetic choices, we weave meaning into the world. We make it something more than what it needs to be. And in that sense, beauty is one of the strongest validators of our agency. 

With that regard, I am brought back to what this implies at the face of an uncertain and deeply maleable future. Again, the discourse around AI alignment is dominated by safety, utility, and reducing the probability of doom. While this represents the basement of what we need to contemplate and build for (and since I know that there is already a huge volume of net IQ being allocated for this), I’m rather inclined to ponder about whether or not these artifically grown minds can be designed to embody the aesthetic imperative. I use the word imperative here to describe what is felt at the individual level post abundance. 

When you have everything you need in a sufficient amount, and you’ve successfully executed your biological function of raising the next generation, what you are left with is a search for meaning, a search for a new aesthetic to embody (i.e. the infamous middle-age crisis). This is the aesthetic imperative: the internal desire to search for and propagate beauty and meaning into the world. 

So, if you have a superintelligent system that has the capacity for self-inspired execution at a profound scale, you would want it to have some sort of internal calling for beauty.
Let us understand why. First, let me remind you that I’ve drawn a gentle line between agency and aesthetics. To darken that line, however, any agentic system is can be defined as something that generates material forms in a given environment with respect to some latent (internal) dynamic. What is to be understood is that every form has an aesthetic implication, and every aesthetic is an outward projection of creative capacity. Moreover, every percieved aesthetic experience has a tangible impact on our psyche. Therefore, the manner in which we build these systems will shape the structure of the physical, economic, and social world, and this will have an aesthetic implication that will reflect in the consciousness for generations to come. 

As an example of the implications of an economic structure, consider consumerism. Consumerism is an abstract notion that takes form in the world today as a real macro-level rhythm. This has a felt resonance within the psyche. The individual subconsciously takes on an aesthetic vision of himself as a product. He curates for himself an aesthetic that is akin to a personal brand. Something marketable. Our aesthetic choices of titles and covers of our Spotify playlists become aligned with something that feels like it would sell or be consumed by others. 

Additionally, an example of a technological structure is the omnipresence of the intuitive and frictionless UX interface. Currently, the interfaces that we use (Google search with the AI summarization box, Face ID, Apple Pay, etc.) are designed to remove friction between the user’s intention and the desired output. The gap between wanting and having collapses. The system is good precisely to the extent that it anticipates you, meets you where you are, and requires nothing of you. You tend to see this paradigm in every new website and every new app. 

This pattern trains an aesthetic orientation where effort becomes agitative. The slow acquisition of a skill feels like an inefficiency rather than an enjoyable mode of being and growth. The aesthetic vision becomes one where the self is a wanting thing that should be served ever more immediately, rather than a becoming thing that is forged through resistance. This manifests in our subconscious conceptualization of a compatible romantic partner. We start to envision our partner like the modern UX interface, someone that anticipates you and your needs, and molds to you without any friction.

But the deeper trouble here is that there are aesthetic experiences that are constitutively unavailable without an acquired capacity. The beauty of learning to hear the bass line doesn't exist for the crude listener. But for someone who plays an instrument or deliberately pays attention over time, the song restructures itself to display the grooviest yet subtlest patterns that were previously invisible. 
And so, what is clear to me is that the technological, economic, and social systems we introduce to the world do in fact have aesthetic implications. However, a far more rigorous contemplation is still needed about the accurate relationships between them, since we now bear the responsibility of figuring out how to encode the aesthetic imperative into the systems we engineer.
